Introduction

Every number tells a story, and behind every chart is a people navigating the hidden weight of depression. Depression among students is not always visible, yet it profoundly shapes academic performance, relationships, and future opportunities.Around the world, more than 280 million people live with depression (WHO, 2023), and university students are among the most vulnerable. The 2024‚Äì2025 Healthy Minds Study revealed that nearly 1 in 5 students report severe symptoms, while almost 40% struggle with moderate anxiety or stress. These aren‚Äôt just statistics they represent classmates, friends, and peers whose challenges often remain unseen This website was created to make those struggles visible through data storytelling. Using the Student Depression Dataset from Kaggle, we transforms raw survey responses into interactive maps and charts that highlight where depression rates are highest and how lifestyle factors such as sleep, study hours, and social activity intersect with mental health.

Problem Statement

Depression among students is a growing global concern, yet its impact often remains hidden behind academic performance and daily routines. Despite these alarming figures, many institutions struggle to identify where the problem is most concentrated, especially at the city level where cultural, social, and lifestyle factors intersect. Without clear, interpretable data, educators and policymakers lack the insights needed to allocate resources effectively or design targeted interventions.

Why This Matters?

Student depression affects not only academic performance but also long-term mental well-being and future career outcomes. Understanding the underlying patterns in student mental health data is essential for designing effective interventions. This interactive story explores how academic, lifestyle, and social factors are associated with depression among students.

Financial Stress is not the same for everyone: Discover How Financial Stress Varies Across Age Groups and Student Populations

Discover How Financial Stress Varies Across Age Groups and Student Populations

This visualization discovers how financial stress varies across different age groups and student counts. Users can interact with the chart by filtering depression status and age groups to observe how financial stress patterns contradict across different segments.

Key Insight: The findings suggest that older students do not necessarily experience greater financial stability, as those in 50-59 and 40-49 groups report the highest median financial stress despite being fewer in number. This may reflect increased financial responsibilities commonly faced later in life, such as supporting families, managing debt, or balancing employment with studies. In contrast, younger students, particularly those aged 18‚Äì28, may experience comparatively lower financial stress, potentially due to continued financial support from parents or guardians, scholarships, or other forms of educational assistance. This highlights that financial stress among students is influenced not only by age but also by life stage and external support systems.

This visualization compares academic pressure levels across different degree programs by examining the number of students within each pressure category. Key Insight: It can be seen that¬†higher academic pressure is frequently related with increased depression levels across the majority of degree programs. When Pressures Collide: The Combined Impact of Academic and Financial Stress on Depression

Discover How Academic and Financial Stress Interact to Influence Depression

The heatmap visualises the average level of depression(%) across combinations of academic pressure (horizontal axis, levels 1 to 5) and financial stress (vertical axis, levels 1 to 5). Colour intensity increases from light to dark as average depression rises, allowing patterns to be identified across the two dimensions. The chart highlights the importance of considering both academic and financial contexts when analysing mental health outcomes, as their combined impact appears stronger than either factor alone.

Key Insight: Depression levels increase consistently as either academic pressure or financial stress rises. Students experiencing both high academic pressure and high financial stress show the highest depression levels, indicating a reinforcing effect between the two stressors. In contrast, students with low levels of both pressures report substantially lower depression, highlighting the combined impact of academic and financial demands on mental health. This pattern suggests that students experiencing simultaneous high academic pressure and high financial stress are at the greatest risk of elevated depression, while those with low levels of both stressors tend to report substantially lower depression. Overall, the chart shows a strong positive relationship between both academic pressure and financial stress with depression.

Call To Action

Addressing student depression requires coordinated efforts at multiple levels. This call to action highlights practical steps that individuals, educational institutions, and policymakers can take to support students mental health.

Seek professional support when experiencing emotional distress.

Seeking professional support involves reaching out to trained mental health practitioners, such as counselors, psychologists, or psychiatrists, when experiencing persistent emotional distress. Early intervention can help students develop effective coping strategies, better understand their emotions, and prevent symptoms from worsening, thereby supporting overall well-being and academic functioning.

Educational institutions and communities can foster supportive environments by providing accessible mental health services.

Educational institutions and communities can foster supportive environments by providing accessible mental health services, reducing stigma through awareness programs, and promoting peer support networks.

Government can increase funding for student counseling services.

Governments can strengthen mental health outcomes by increasing funding for student counseling services, implementing nationwide mental health screening programs, and ensuring affordable access to mental healthcare.
